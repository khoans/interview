**46.** Tell me about a time you had to work with a difficult team member. How did you handle it?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

I focus on understanding their perspective first and finding common ground on deliverables rather than trying to change their personality.

On the automotive marketplace, I worked with a senior engineer who was very protective of the legacy Solr search code and resistant to the microservices refactoring effort. Every PR I submitted for the search service got extensive pushback. Instead of escalating, I scheduled a 1-on-1 and asked him to walk me through the Solr query pipeline — his deep knowledge was genuinely impressive. I acknowledged his expertise, then framed the refactoring as preserving his good design while making it testable and independently deployable. I incorporated his suggestions on the Solr query abstraction layer. Over a few weeks, he became one of the strongest advocates for the new architecture because he felt ownership over it.

The lesson: "difficult" often means "not heard." Most resistance comes from feeling excluded from decisions, not from being unreasonable. The trade-off is it takes more time upfront, but you avoid months of passive resistance.
