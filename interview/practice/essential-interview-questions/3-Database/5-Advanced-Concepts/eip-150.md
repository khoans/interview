## 150. What is the difference between optimistic and pessimistic locking?

**Optimistic locking assumes conflicts are rare — check version before commit. Pessimistic locking assumes conflicts are common — lock the row upfront.**

Choose based on your read-to-write ratio and conflict frequency.

**Optimistic locking:**
- Read data with version number, check version on write
- If version changed, transaction fails — retry with fresh data
- No database locks — concurrent reads don't block
- Best for: read-heavy workloads (90%+ reads), rare conflicts

**Pessimistic locking:**
- Lock row when reading (`SELECT ... FOR UPDATE`)
- Other transactions wait until lock released
- Guarantees no conflicts — exclusive access
- Best for: write-heavy workloads, high contention, expensive retries

**Trade-off:**
- Optimistic: higher concurrency, but retries cost CPU and latency
- Pessimistic: no retries, but lock contention kills throughput under load
- Wrong choice causes either timeout storms (pessimistic) or retry loops (optimistic)

**Real-world example:**
Our inventory system used pessimistic locking — `SELECT ... FOR UPDATE` on stock row. During flash sales, 1000 concurrent users — 990 waited for locks, timeouts spiked to 40%. Switched to optimistic locking with `@Version`. 95% succeeded first try, 5% retried once. Zero timeouts, throughput increased 5x. But for fund transfers (high contention, money movement), we kept pessimistic locking — retries unacceptable for financial transactions. Different use cases, different strategies.

**Rule of thumb:** Optimistic for 90% of cases — especially read-heavy. Pessimistic for high-contention writes or when retries are unacceptable (financial, regulatory). Monitor retry rates — above 10% means reconsider your choice.
