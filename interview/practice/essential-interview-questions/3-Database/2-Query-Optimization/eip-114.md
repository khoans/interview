## 114. What is an execution plan?

**An execution plan is the database's blueprint for how it will execute a query — which indexes to use, join order, access methods, and estimated costs.**

It's the optimizer's decision on the most efficient way to retrieve your data.

**What it shows:**
- **Access method** — index seek, index scan, or full table scan
- **Join strategy** — nested loop, hash join, or merge join
- **Join order** — which tables are processed first
- **Estimated vs. actual rows** — reveals stale statistics
- **Cost percentage** — which operations are most expensive

**Why it matters:**
- Explains why a query is slow (full scan instead of index seek)
- Reveals optimizer mistakes (wrong join order, ignored indexes)
- Guides optimization efforts — fix the 80% cost operation first

**Trade-off:**
- Estimated plans may differ from actual execution — always verify with runtime stats
- Complex queries have massive plans — 100+ operators, hard to interpret
- Plans change with data distribution — what's optimal today may not be tomorrow

**Real-world example:**
Our order report took 30 seconds — 5 table JOINs, looked correct. Execution plan revealed the issue: optimizer chose nested loop joins (good for small data) but was joining 100K rows — 10 billion comparisons. Estimated rows: 500. Actual rows: 100K. Statistics were 6 months stale. Ran `UPDATE STATISTICS`, optimizer switched to hash joins. Query dropped to 800ms (37x faster). Now we update statistics weekly and check estimated vs. actual rows for critical queries.

**Rule of thumb:** Always check actual execution plans for slow queries. Focus on operators with highest cost. Verify estimated vs. actual rows match — big gaps mean stale statistics.
