## 138. When would you denormalize?

**Denormalize when read performance is critical and the cost of JOINs outweighs the cost of data redundancy.**

It's a deliberate trade-off: sacrifice write efficiency and some consistency for faster reads.

**Valid reasons to denormalize:**
- Read-heavy workloads — 90%+ reads, 10% writes
- Performance-critical queries — sub-100ms requirements
- Reporting/analytics — aggregations across many tables
- Caching layer exists — denormalized cache, normalized source of truth

**Common patterns:**
- Embedding — customer name in orders (avoid JOIN for display)
- Summary columns — order_total in orders table (avoid SUM for every query)
- Duplicate foreign keys — category_name in products (avoid JOIN to categories)

**Trade-off:**
- Reads are 5-10x faster — no JOINs, denormalized data ready
- Writes are slower — update multiple tables, maintain consistency
- Risk of inconsistency — application must keep duplicates in sync

**Real-world example:**
Our e-commerce product page needed 8 JOINs (product, category, brand, supplier, specs, images, reviews, inventory). Query: 400ms. Target: <100ms. Denormalized: embedded category_name, brand_name, supplier_name in products table. Added summary columns: avg_rating, review_count, stock_status. Query: 60ms (6.6x faster). Write complexity: product update now updates 3 tables, but writes are 1% of traffic. Zero customer-facing slowdowns since.

**Rule of thumb:** Start normalized. Profile queries. Denormalize only the 10% of queries causing 90% of latency. Document denormalization — future developers need to know why redundancy exists.
