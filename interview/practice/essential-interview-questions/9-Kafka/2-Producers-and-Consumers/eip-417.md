## 417. What is the difference between acks=0, acks=1, and acks=all?

**acks=0: no acknowledgment (fastest, data loss risk). acks=1: leader acknowledges (balanced). acks=all: all replicas acknowledge (safest, slowest).**

Different durability guarantees for different use cases.

**Comparison:**
| Acks | Durability | Latency | Use Case |
|------|------------|---------|----------|
| 0 | None — message may be lost | 1ms | Metrics, debug logs |
| 1 | Leader durability — lost if leader fails before replication | 5ms | Most applications |
| all | Full durability — survives leader failure | 15ms | Financial, critical data |

**Trade-off:**
- acks=0: 10x throughput of acks=all — but data loss on any failure
- acks=all: 100% durability — but 3x latency of acks=1
- acks=1: sweet spot for most — leader failure is rare

**Real-world example:**
Our payment system used acks=1 — leader acknowledges payment processed. Leader broker failed before replication — payment lost, customer charged twice (retry). Financial impact: $50K in double charges. Migrated to acks=all — all replicas acknowledge. Payment loss: eliminated. Latency: 5ms → 15ms. For payments, 15ms is acceptable. For click tracking, acks=1 is fine — occasional lost click is acceptable.

**Rule of thumb:** acks=all for financial transactions — no data loss. acks=1 for most applications — balanced. acks=0 only for truly disposable data — metrics, logs.
