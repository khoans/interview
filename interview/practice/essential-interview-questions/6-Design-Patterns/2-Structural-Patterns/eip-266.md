## 266. What is the Facade pattern?

**Facade pattern provides simplified interface to complex subsystem — single entry point hiding complexity.**

It's about making complex systems easy to use — one method call instead of ten.

**When to use:**
- Complex subsystems — 5+ classes to accomplish simple task
- Layer isolation — hide subsystem changes from callers
- Legacy modernization — new simple facade over old complex code

**Trade-off:**
- Simplicity — one method vs. understanding entire subsystem
- But: facade becomes god class — knows everything, does everything
- Limited flexibility — facade provides common path, not all capabilities

**Real-world example:**
Our video processing required 8 steps: validate format, extract audio, transcode video, merge, add watermark, compress, upload to CDN, notify. Callers orchestrated all 8 steps — 100 lines per caller, bugs everywhere. Created VideoProcessingFacade.process(video): one method, 100 lines inside facade. Callers: 100 lines → 1 line. Bug rate: 5/month → 0. But facade became 500 lines — hard to test. Split into Facade (orchestration) + Strategy classes (each step). Testability restored.

**Rule of thumb:** Facade for complex subsystems with common workflows. Not for simple operations — over-engineering. Keep facade thin — delegate to subsystem classes, don't implement logic in facade.
