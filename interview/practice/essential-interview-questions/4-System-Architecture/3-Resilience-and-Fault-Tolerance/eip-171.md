## 171. What happens if one microservice is down?

**It depends on your resilience patterns — without protection, cascading failures take down the entire system. With proper patterns, the failure is contained and users see degraded functionality, not errors.**

The whole point of microservices is failure isolation — but that doesn't happen automatically.

**Without resilience:**
- Synchronous calls block until timeout (30-60 seconds)
- Thread pools exhaust waiting for responses
- Caller service fails, propagates to its callers
- Cascade: one service down → entire system unavailable

**With resilience:**
- Circuit breaker opens after failures — fast-fail instead of timeout
- Fallback returns cached data or graceful error
- Bulkhead isolates failures — one service doesn't exhaust all threads
- Users see "recommendations unavailable" not "site is down"

**Trade-off:**
- Resilience patterns add complexity — circuit breaker tuning, fallback logic
- But: 99.9% availability vs. 95% without protection
- Users tolerate partial degradation, not complete outages

**Real-world example:**
Our payment service went down during deployment (10 minutes). Without circuit breaker: checkout hung 60 seconds per request, thread pools exhausted, cart service failed, entire site unavailable. Revenue loss: $50K. Next deployment: added circuit breaker (5 failures → open, 30-second timeout). Payment service down: checkout shows "Payment temporarily unavailable, try again" — 2-second error, not 60-second hang. Zero cascade failures since.

**Rule of thumb:** Assume every service will fail. Circuit breakers for synchronous calls, retries with backoff for transient failures, fallbacks for non-critical features. Test failure scenarios — chaos engineering quarterly.
