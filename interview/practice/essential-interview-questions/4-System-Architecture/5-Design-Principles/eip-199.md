## 199. What is two-phase commit?

**Two-phase commit (2PC) is a distributed transaction protocol that ensures all participants commit or all abort — atomic commits across multiple databases or services.**

It's strong consistency at the cost of performance and availability.

**Two phases:**
- **Phase 1 (Prepare)** — coordinator asks all participants "can you commit?" Each locks resources, responds yes/no
- **Phase 2 (Commit)** — if all said yes, coordinator sends "commit" to all. If any said no, sends "rollback" to all

**Trade-off:**
- Atomic commits — all-or-nothing across distributed systems
- But: blocking protocol — participants hold locks until coordinator decides
- Single point of failure — coordinator crash leaves participants in limbo
- Performance — 2 round-trips, locks held throughout

**Real-world example:**
Our fund transfer: debit from account A (Bank A), credit to account B (Bank B). Without 2PC: debit succeeded, credit failed — $5K vanished. Implemented 2PC: both banks prepare, both commit or both rollback. Zero lost transfers in 3 years. But: 2PC took 500ms (2 network round-trips + lock wait). During coordinator outage (10 minutes), 100 transfers stuck in "prepared" state — accounts debited, not credited, locks held. Manual intervention required. Switched to saga pattern for most transfers — eventual consistency, compensating transactions. Kept 2PC only for high-value transfers (> $100K).

**Rule of thumb:** 2PC for high-value, low-volume transactions requiring strong consistency. Saga for high-volume, can-tolerate-eventual-consistency workflows. Avoid 2PC for user-facing operations — latency and blocking aren't worth it.
