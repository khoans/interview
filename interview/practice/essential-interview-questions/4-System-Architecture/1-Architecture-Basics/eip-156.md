## 156. What is service-oriented architecture (SOA)?

**SOA is an architectural style where services communicate over a network using standardized protocols — predecessor to microservices, but with centralized governance and shared infrastructure.**

It's microservices' enterprise cousin — more governance, more standardization, more complexity.

**Key characteristics:**
- Services with well-defined interfaces (SOAP, WSDL, XML)
- Enterprise service bus (ESB) for routing, transformation, orchestration
- Centralized governance — shared standards, contracts, security
- Service registry — discoverable services with versioning

**Trade-off:**
- Standardization — consistent interfaces across enterprise
- But: ESB becomes bottleneck, single point of failure
- Reusability — services designed for enterprise consumption
- But: over-engineering, services try to serve everyone

**Real-world example:**
Our enterprise had 50 applications, each with custom integrations — 200+ point-to-point connections. Implemented SOA with ESB — standardized on SOAP/WSDL, centralized routing. Integration time dropped from 3 months to 2 weeks. But ESB became bottleneck — 80% of traffic went through it, latency spiked to 500ms. ESB outage took down 40 applications. Migrated to microservices — lightweight REST APIs, no ESB, direct service-to-service communication. Latency: 500ms → 50ms.

**Rule of thumb:** SOA works for large enterprises with legacy integration needs. For greenfield applications, microservices without ESB are simpler and faster. Don't over-engineer unless you have 20+ applications to integrate.
