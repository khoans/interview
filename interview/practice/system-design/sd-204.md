**204.** What is a resource-based policy vs identity-based policy in AWS?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Identity-based policies are attached to IAM users, groups, or roles -- they define what that identity can do. Resource-based policies are attached to the resource itself (S3 bucket, SQS queue, KMS key) -- they define who can access that resource. The key difference: resource-based policies can grant cross-account access without AssumeRole, and they specify a Principal (who).

In our marketplace: (1) Identity-based -- our listing-service IAM role had a policy allowing s3:PutObject on the vehicle-images bucket. (2) Resource-based -- the vehicle-images S3 bucket had a bucket policy allowing the CloudFront distribution to read objects (OAI principal), and a policy allowing a partner account to write listing data. (3) KMS key policy (resource-based) controlled which roles could encrypt/decrypt with our data encryption key. When both policy types exist, AWS evaluates them together -- the request must be allowed by at least one policy and not explicitly denied by any.

The trade-off: resource-based policies enable simpler cross-account access (no role chaining needed) but they live on the resource, making it harder to audit permissions centrally. With identity-based policies, you can see all permissions from the IAM console. We used AWS IAM Access Analyzer to detect unintended cross-account access through resource policies -- it flagged an S3 bucket that accidentally had a wildcard principal.
