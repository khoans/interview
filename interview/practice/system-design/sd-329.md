**329.** When should you use a time-series database (InfluxDB, TimescaleDB) vs storing metrics in PostgreSQL?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Use a time-series database when you have high-frequency ingestion (thousands of writes/second), need efficient time-range queries with downsampling, and automatic data retention policies. Use PostgreSQL when your metrics volume is low and you do not want another database to manage.

On our marketplace, we stored application metrics in New Relic (SaaS time-series backend) and business metrics (daily listing counts, price trends, dealer activity) in PostgreSQL because the volume was low — one row per dealer per day, aggregated by nightly batch jobs. PostgreSQL handled it fine with proper partitioning by date. If we had been ingesting per-request metrics at 10,000+ writes/second, PostgreSQL would have struggled and a dedicated time-series database like TimescaleDB (PostgreSQL extension) would have been the right choice.

The trade-off: time-series databases excel at write throughput, time-range compression, and automatic downsampling (keep 1-second resolution for a week, then aggregate to 1-minute for a year). But they add operational complexity and are single-purpose — you cannot join time-series data with your relational data easily. If your volume is under a few hundred writes/second, PostgreSQL with partitioning handles it fine without the extra infrastructure.
