**59.** What is chaos engineering? How does it improve system reliability?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Chaos engineering is deliberately injecting failures into a production (or staging) system to test its resilience — killing instances, introducing network latency, filling disks. The goal is to find weaknesses before they cause real outages. Netflix's Chaos Monkey is the most famous example — randomly terminates production instances during business hours.

In our project, we ran chaos experiments in our staging environment. We simulated: (1) Killing a random ECS instance during load testing — validated that the ALB rerouted traffic within 30 seconds and auto-scaling replaced the instance. (2) Adding 2-second latency to the pricing service — verified that the circuit breaker in the listing service opened correctly and served cached prices. (3) Failing the RDS primary — confirmed Multi-AZ failover completed in ~90 seconds and our connection pool recovered. The most valuable finding: when we killed a Redis node, we discovered our services didn't gracefully degrade — they threw exceptions to users instead of falling back to database queries. We fixed this by adding a Redis fallback path.

The trade-off: chaos engineering in production carries real risk — a poorly designed experiment can cause actual outages. It also requires observability maturity (you need good monitoring to detect the impact). We only ran experiments in staging because the business risk of production chaos was too high for our team's maturity level. The lesson: start chaos engineering in staging. Graduate to production only after you have confidence in your observability and rollback mechanisms. The biggest value is discovering unknown failure modes before customers do.
