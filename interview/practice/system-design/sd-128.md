**128.** What is DNS poisoning / DNS spoofing? How do you prevent it (DNSSEC)?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
DNS poisoning injects false DNS records into a resolver's cache, redirecting users to malicious servers. An attacker poisons the DNS response for your domain, and users are silently sent to a phishing site. DNSSEC (DNS Security Extensions) prevents this by digitally signing DNS records — resolvers verify the signature chain from the root zone down, rejecting forged responses.

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, we used Route 53 for DNS management. Route 53 supports DNSSEC signing for hosted zones, which we enabled for our primary domain. This meant DNS responses for our domain were signed with a KSK (Key Signing Key) managed by Route 53, and resolvers supporting DNSSEC validation could verify the responses were authentic. Additionally, we used Route 53 health checks to detect if our DNS records were returning unexpected IPs — an alert fired if the resolved IP did not match our known CloudFront or ALB IP ranges.

**Trade-off:** DNSSEC adds complexity (key management, zone signing, DS record delegation) and slightly increases DNS response sizes due to signatures. Not all resolvers validate DNSSEC, so it is not a complete solution. Also, DNSSEC proves authenticity of DNS records but does not encrypt DNS queries — DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) or DNS-over-TLS (DoT) provide query privacy. For most applications, DNSSEC plus TLS is sufficient — TLS certificate validation is the final guard against DNS poisoning even without DNSSEC.
