**50.** What is leader election? How do algorithms like Raft and Paxos work at a high level?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Leader election is the process of choosing one node in a distributed cluster to be the coordinator (leader) that makes decisions, while others (followers) replicate those decisions. Raft works in terms: a follower becomes a candidate after a timeout, requests votes from other nodes, and wins if it gets a majority. The leader then sends heartbeats to maintain authority. If the leader dies, a new election starts. Paxos is older and more theoretical — it achieves consensus through a propose-accept-commit protocol requiring majority agreement.

In our project, we didn't implement leader election directly, but we relied on systems that use it. Our RDS PostgreSQL Multi-AZ deployment used leader election internally — when the primary failed, AWS promoted the standby replica to primary (automated failover in ~60 seconds). Our ElastiCache Redis cluster used a similar mechanism for primary failover. The most visible impact was during an RDS failover incident — the primary node had a hardware issue, and the automatic leader election promoted the standby. Our services experienced ~90 seconds of connection errors before the new primary was ready and DNS updated. We handled this with connection retry logic in HikariCP.

The trade-off: leader election guarantees consistency (single source of truth) but creates a temporary unavailability window during election. Leaderless designs (like DynamoDB, Cassandra) avoid this but require conflict resolution. The lesson: if you use managed services (RDS, ElastiCache), leader election is handled for you — just make sure your application handles the failover window gracefully with retries and connection pool recovery.
