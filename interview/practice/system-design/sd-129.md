**129.** What is a DDoS attack? Explain L3/L4 vs L7 DDoS attacks and their mitigations.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
A DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attack overwhelms a service with traffic from many sources. L3/L4 attacks (SYN floods, UDP floods, ICMP floods) target network/transport layers with raw volume to exhaust bandwidth and connection tables. L7 attacks target the application layer with legitimate-looking HTTP requests that are expensive to process (e.g., complex search queries, large form submissions).

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, we experienced both types. An L4 SYN flood was mitigated automatically by AWS Shield Standard — we only noticed it in Shield metrics after the fact. More challenging was an L7 attack: bots sent thousands of complex vehicle search queries with many filters simultaneously, each hitting Solr with expensive faceted searches. Our auto-scaling kicked in, but the attack cost us in ECS compute bills. We mitigated it with WAF rate limiting rules (100 requests/minute per IP for the search endpoint), CloudFront geographic restrictions (blocked traffic from countries with no users), and added CAPTCHA challenges for suspicious traffic patterns.

**Trade-off:** L3/L4 mitigation is largely solved by cloud providers (Shield, CloudFront absorb volumetric attacks at the edge). L7 mitigation is harder because requests look legitimate — rate limiting too aggressively blocks real users, too loosely allows the attack through. We tuned WAF rules iteratively using CloudWatch metrics to find the right threshold, and used bot detection (AWS WAF Bot Control) to distinguish automated from human traffic.
