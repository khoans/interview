**43.** What is the saga pattern? How does it handle distributed transactions?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

A saga is a sequence of local transactions across multiple services, where each step has a compensating action to undo its work if a later step fails. Unlike a distributed 2-phase commit (which locks resources), sagas use eventual consistency — each service commits independently. Two styles: choreography (services react to events) and orchestration (a central coordinator directs the flow).

In our project, we used a saga for the "dealer purchases featured listing" flow. Step 1: Payment service charges the dealer's card. Step 2: Listing service upgrades the listing to featured status. Step 3: Search service boosts the listing's ranking. If step 3 failed (search index unavailable), the compensating actions rolled back: listing service reverted to standard status, payment service issued a refund. We used orchestration via a Lambda function that coordinated the steps — it was easier to understand and debug than choreography where events bounce between services and the flow is implicit.

The trade-off: choreography is more decoupled (no central coordinator to be a SPOF) but harder to debug — the saga's state is spread across multiple services' event handlers. Orchestration is easier to understand and monitor but the orchestrator is a single point of failure. The lesson: use orchestration for complex sagas (5+ steps) where visibility matters, choreography for simple 2-3 step flows. Always design compensating actions upfront — they're not an afterthought.
