**127.** What is a man-in-the-middle (MITM) attack? How does TLS prevent it?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
A MITM attack occurs when an attacker intercepts communication between two parties, potentially reading or altering messages while both parties believe they are communicating directly. TLS prevents this through certificate verification (proving the server is who it claims to be), encrypted communication (attacker cannot read intercepted data), and integrity checks (tampering is detected via MAC).

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, TLS protected all user-facing traffic. When a buyer visited our site, their browser verified our TLS certificate (issued by ACM, signed by Amazon CA) before sending any data. If an attacker on a coffee shop WiFi tried to intercept the connection with a fake certificate, the browser would reject it because it was not signed by a trusted CA. We also enforced HSTS headers (Strict-Transport-Security) so browsers refused to connect over plain HTTP at all, preventing SSL stripping attacks where an attacker downgrades HTTPS to HTTP.

**Trade-off:** TLS prevents network-level MITM but does not protect against a compromised CA issuing a fraudulent certificate for your domain. Certificate Transparency (CT) logs help detect rogue certificates — browsers now require CT for all new certificates. Also, corporate proxies that install their own root CA on employee devices can technically MITM TLS traffic for inspection, which is a legitimate enterprise use case but undermines end-user privacy.
