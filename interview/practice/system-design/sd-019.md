**19.** What is connection pooling? Why is it important for database performance?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Connection pooling maintains a pool of reusable database connections instead of creating a new connection per request. Creating a TCP connection to a database involves DNS lookup, TCP handshake, TLS negotiation, and authentication — that's 20-50ms overhead per connection. A pool keeps connections warm and ready.

In our project, we used HikariCP (Spring Boot's default) with a pool size of 20 per service instance. With 8 instances of our listing service running, that was 160 connections to PostgreSQL. We tuned this carefully because RDS had a max_connections limit of 500. Before pooling optimization, we had an incident where a traffic spike caused connection exhaustion — services waited for connections, requests queued up, and response times spiked from 100ms to 10 seconds. After tuning, we set connectionTimeout to 3 seconds (fail fast instead of wait forever) and maxLifetime to 30 minutes (recycle connections before RDS kills idle ones).

The trade-off: too small a pool means requests queue up waiting for connections; too large wastes database memory and can exceed max_connections. The formula that worked for us: pool_size = (core_count * 2) + effective_spindle_count. For most services, 10-20 was the sweet spot. The lesson: monitor your pool's active/idle/waiting metrics — we used HikariCP's built-in metrics exported to New Relic.
