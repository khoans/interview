**201.** What is IAM (Identity and Access Management)? Explain users, roles, and policies in AWS.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

IAM is the AWS service that controls who (authentication) can do what (authorization) on which resources. Users are human identities with long-lived credentials. Roles are assumable identities for services, applications, or cross-account access -- no permanent credentials, just temporary tokens. Policies are JSON documents defining permissions (allow/deny specific actions on specific resources).

In our marketplace: IAM Users were for our 8 developers (each with MFA required, console access for debugging). IAM Roles were for services -- each ECS task had a task role (listing-service-role with DynamoDB and S3 access), and each EC2 instance had an instance profile. Policies followed least privilege: the listing-service policy allowed dynamodb:PutItem and dynamodb:GetItem on arn:aws:dynamodb:*:*:table/listings only. We used AWS managed policies for common patterns (ReadOnlyAccess for junior devs) and custom policies for service-specific access. IAM Groups (backend-developers, devops) simplified policy assignment.

The trade-off: IAM policies are powerful but complex -- conflicting allow/deny across multiple policies creates unexpected behavior. We once had a developer unable to access a resource because an SCP (Service Control Policy) denied it at the organization level, despite their IAM policy allowing it. Use IAM Policy Simulator and Access Analyzer to debug permission issues.
