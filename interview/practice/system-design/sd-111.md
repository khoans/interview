**111.** What is AES-256? Why is it the industry standard for encryption at rest?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
AES-256 is the Advanced Encryption Standard with a 256-bit key, providing 2^256 possible keys — computationally infeasible to brute-force even with future quantum computers (Grover's algorithm reduces it to 2^128, still secure). It is fast in hardware (Intel AES-NI instruction set), standardized by NIST, and battle-tested for 20+ years.

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, AES-256 was used everywhere for data at rest. S3 buckets used SSE-KMS (AES-256 under the hood) for vehicle documents. RDS PostgreSQL used AES-256 for storage encryption. DynamoDB used AES-256 for table-level encryption. Even our EBS volumes on ECS instances were AES-256 encrypted. AWS handles the actual AES operations transparently — our Spring Boot services never dealt with encryption algorithms directly; we just enabled encryption in the resource configuration and KMS managed the keys.

**Trade-off:** AES-256 is marginally slower than AES-128 (~40% more rounds: 14 vs 10), but on modern hardware with AES-NI, the difference is negligible (both exceed 1 GB/s throughput). The real cost is key management, not the algorithm itself. For compliance requirements (FIPS 140-2, PCI DSS), AES-256 is often mandated regardless of the performance difference, making it the safe default choice.
