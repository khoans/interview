**310.** When should you merge two microservices back into one? What are the signs of over-decomposition?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Merge when: two services always deploy together, every change in one requires a change in the other, they share a database, or they cannot function independently. These are signs of over-decomposition — you split on an artificial boundary.

On our marketplace, we had a listing-detail service and a listing-media service that were initially separate. But every listing update required both services to change simultaneously, they shared the same PostgreSQL schema, and a failure in media broke the detail page entirely. We merged them into a single listings service. Deployments went from coordinated 2-service releases to simple single-service deploys, and latency dropped because we eliminated the internal HTTP call between them.

The trade-off: merging feels like going backward, and teams resist it. But the real cost of over-decomposition is operational — more deployments to coordinate, more network hops, more failure points, and distributed debugging for what is fundamentally one domain. Two services that are always coupled are worse than one service that is well-structured internally.
