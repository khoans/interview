**57.** What is a retry strategy? Explain exponential backoff with jitter.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

A retry strategy re-attempts failed requests to handle transient failures (network blips, temporary overloads). Exponential backoff increases the wait time between retries exponentially (1s, 2s, 4s, 8s) to avoid overwhelming a recovering service. Jitter adds randomness to the backoff interval so multiple clients don't retry simultaneously (thundering herd on retry).

In our project, we configured retry with exponential backoff and jitter on all inter-service calls using Resilience4j. When the listing service called the pricing service and got a 503, it retried with delays of ~1s, ~2s, ~4s (with random jitter of +/- 500ms). Without jitter, we had an incident where a pricing service restart caused all 8 listing service instances to retry at exactly the same intervals — 8 synchronized retry waves hit the recovering pricing service and kept it overloaded. Adding jitter spread the retries randomly, letting the pricing service recover gradually. We also implemented per-exception retry policies: retry on 503/timeout, don't retry on 400/404 (those won't succeed on retry).

The trade-off: retries increase load on an already-struggling service — without backoff and jitter, retries can make outages worse (retry storm). Too many retries with long backoffs increase end-to-end latency for the user. We capped at 3 retries with a maximum 8-second total retry window. The lesson: always use exponential backoff WITH jitter. Never retry with fixed intervals. And set a retry budget — if the circuit breaker opens, stop retrying entirely.
