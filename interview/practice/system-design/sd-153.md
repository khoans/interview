**153.** What is the principle of least privilege? How do you apply it to services and users?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

The principle of least privilege means every user, service, or process should have only the minimum permissions needed to do its job -- nothing more. This limits the blast radius if a credential is compromised.

In our marketplace, each microservice on ECS had its own IAM role scoped to exactly what it needed. The listing service role could read/write to the listings DynamoDB table and the S3 images bucket, but had zero access to the user database or payment systems. Our developers had read-only AWS console access by default; only the DevOps lead had production write access via a separate IAM role that required MFA to assume. At the database level, our Spring Boot services connected with PostgreSQL users that had only SELECT/INSERT/UPDATE on their specific schemas -- no DROP, no access to other schemas.

The trade-off: granular permissions are tedious to manage -- we had around 15 IAM roles across services. But when a developer accidentally exposed a service credential in a log, the damage was contained to one DynamoDB table instead of the entire AWS account.
