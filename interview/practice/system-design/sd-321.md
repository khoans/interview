**321.** SQL vs NoSQL: What specific questions should you ask about your data to make this decision?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Ask five questions: (1) Do I need complex joins and transactions? SQL. (2) Is my schema well-defined or will it evolve frequently? Fixed schema favors SQL, flexible favors NoSQL. (3) What are my read/write patterns? Read-heavy with complex queries favors SQL; write-heavy with simple lookups favors NoSQL. (4) What scale do I need? NoSQL scales horizontally more easily. (5) Do I need ACID guarantees? SQL.

On our marketplace, we used both. Vehicle listings, dealer accounts, and transactions lived in PostgreSQL because they had complex relationships (listing belongs to dealer, has pricing history, located at address), required joins, and needed ACID transactions for financial data. But user session data went to DynamoDB because it was simple key-value lookups, needed millisecond latency at massive scale (30M users), and had no relational requirements. Using PostgreSQL for sessions would have been expensive and slow; using DynamoDB for listings with complex search would have been painful.

The trade-off: SQL gives you data integrity and query flexibility but is harder to scale horizontally. NoSQL gives you scale and speed for simple access patterns but forces you to denormalize and loses join capability. The right answer is almost always "use both for different use cases."
