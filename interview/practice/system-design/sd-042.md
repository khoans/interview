**42.** What is the circuit breaker pattern? How does it prevent cascading failures?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

A circuit breaker monitors calls to a downstream service. When failures exceed a threshold, it "opens" the circuit — subsequent calls fail immediately without hitting the downstream service, giving it time to recover. After a cooldown period, it allows a few test requests through (half-open state). If they succeed, the circuit closes and normal traffic resumes.

In our project, we implemented circuit breakers using Resilience4j in our Spring Boot services. The listing service called the pricing service synchronously on every page load. When the pricing service had a deployment issue and started returning 500 errors, without a circuit breaker, all listing page requests would wait for the pricing timeout (5 seconds), queue up, and eventually exhaust the thread pool — taking down the listing service too. With the circuit breaker configured at 50% failure rate threshold over a 10-second sliding window, it opened after detecting the pricing service was failing. The listing service returned cached prices (stale but available) instead of waiting for the broken pricing service. This prevented a localized pricing issue from cascading into a full site outage.

The trade-off: circuit breakers add complexity — you need to define fallback behavior for every downstream call (what do you show when the pricing service is down?). Setting thresholds too aggressively can open the circuit on transient blips; too loosely and it doesn't protect fast enough. The lesson: pair circuit breakers with meaningful fallbacks. "Return cached data" is a great fallback. "Return an error" still fails the user — the circuit breaker just fails faster.
