**113.** What is a digital certificate? How does the certificate chain of trust work?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
A digital certificate binds a public key to an identity (domain name, organization), signed by a Certificate Authority (CA). The chain of trust works hierarchically: a root CA (trusted by browsers/OS) signs intermediate CA certificates, which sign end-entity (server) certificates. Your browser trusts the root, so it transitively trusts the server certificate.

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, AWS Certificate Manager (ACM) issued TLS certificates for our domains. The chain was: Amazon Root CA 1 (in browser trust stores) signed Amazon Intermediate CA, which signed our *.autotrader-marketplace.com certificate. When a user's browser connected, it received our server cert plus the intermediate cert, verified the chain up to the trusted root, and established the TLS connection. ACM handled auto-renewal 30 days before expiration, which prevented the certificate expiry outage we once had with a manually managed cert on an older service.

**Trade-off:** Certificate management at scale is operationally complex. Expired certificates cause immediate outages (browsers refuse to connect). ACM auto-renewal solved this for ALB/CloudFront, but certificates for internal services (mTLS between microservices) still required monitoring. We set up New Relic alerts for certificates expiring within 14 days as a safety net.
