**264.** What is a brute force attack? How do account lockout and rate limiting help?

A brute force attack systematically tries all possible passwords until finding the correct one. Account lockout temporarily disables the account after N failed attempts. Rate limiting restricts the number of requests per time window from a single source.

On our marketplace, we implemented multiple layers: Spring Security locked accounts after 5 failed login attempts for 15 minutes, our ALB rate-limited login endpoints to 10 requests per minute per IP, and we used CAPTCHA after 3 failed attempts. We also monitored failed login patterns in Kibana -- a spike in failed logins across multiple accounts from the same IP range indicated a credential stuffing attack rather than a single brute force attempt, triggering automated IP blocking at the CloudFront WAF level.

The trade-off: aggressive lockout policies enable denial-of-service -- an attacker can intentionally lock out legitimate users by trying bad passwords against their accounts. We balanced this by using progressive delays (1 min, 5 min, 15 min) instead of hard lockouts, and allowing unlock via email verification. Rate limiting by IP is also imperfect since attackers use distributed botnets.
