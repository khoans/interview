**56.** What is backpressure? How do you handle it when a producer is faster than a consumer?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Backpressure is a mechanism to slow down producers when consumers can't keep up, preventing unbounded queue growth and eventual out-of-memory crashes. Strategies include: blocking the producer until the consumer catches up, dropping messages (load shedding), buffering with bounded queues, and scaling consumers dynamically.

In our project, we encountered backpressure with our image processing pipeline. Dealers could bulk-upload hundreds of vehicle photos. The upload service accepted images fast and published to an SQS queue, but the image processing consumers (resizing, watermarking, optimization) were CPU-intensive and slower. During peak upload hours, the queue depth grew to 50K+ messages. We handled this three ways: (1) SQS as a buffer — SQS can hold unlimited messages, so the queue absorbed the burst without losing data. (2) Auto-scaling consumers — CloudWatch alarm on queue depth triggered ECS to scale image processors from 4 to 12 instances. (3) Backpressure to the producer — if the queue exceeded 100K messages, the upload API returned 503 with "try again later" instead of accepting more uploads.

The trade-off: buffering (SQS) is simplest but processing latency increases during bursts — dealers waited longer for their photos to appear. Scaling consumers is reactive (takes minutes to spin up). Blocking the producer is fastest relief but degrades the user experience. The lesson: use bounded buffers as the first defense, auto-scale consumers as the second, and only apply producer backpressure as a last resort to prevent system collapse.
