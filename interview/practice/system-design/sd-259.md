**259.** What is bandwidth vs throughput? Why can actual throughput be lower than bandwidth?

Bandwidth is the maximum theoretical capacity of a network link (e.g., 1 Gbps). Throughput is the actual data transfer rate achieved in practice. Throughput is almost always lower than bandwidth due to: protocol overhead (TCP headers, TLS encryption), congestion (shared links), packet loss (requiring retransmissions), and latency (TCP window size limits throughput over high-latency links).

On our marketplace, our ECS instances had network bandwidth up to 10 Gbps on c5.xlarge instances, but actual throughput during peak traffic was much lower because: TLS encryption added overhead, small API responses (JSON payloads of 1-5KB) meant per-request overhead dominated, and TCP congestion control throttled during bursts. For our vehicle image uploads to S3, we used multipart uploads to maximize throughput by parallelizing the transfer across multiple TCP connections.

The trade-off: you can increase throughput by using larger packet sizes (jumbo frames in VPC), persistent connections (HTTP keep-alive), and compression (gzip on API responses). We enabled gzip in Spring Boot and CloudFront, reducing our JSON response sizes by 70% and effectively increasing throughput for the same bandwidth.
