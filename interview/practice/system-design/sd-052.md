**52.** What is redundancy? Explain active-passive vs active-active configurations.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Redundancy means having backup components ready to take over if the primary fails. Active-passive: the standby sits idle and only activates when the primary fails (simple, but wastes resources). Active-active: all instances serve traffic simultaneously — if one fails, others absorb its load (efficient, but requires stateless design and data synchronization).

In our project, we used active-active for stateless services and active-passive for stateful components. Our ECS services were active-active — 8 listing service instances all handled traffic simultaneously behind an ALB. If 2 instances failed, the remaining 6 absorbed the load (with auto-scaling adding replacements within minutes). Our PostgreSQL database was active-passive — one primary handled writes, a standby replica in another AZ took over only during failover. We also ran active-passive across regions: us-east-1 was the primary region serving all traffic, us-west-2 had a warm standby with read replicas and pre-configured infrastructure, ready for manual failover during a regional disaster.

The trade-off: active-active is more efficient (no idle resources) and provides instant failover, but requires all nodes to be in sync — for databases, this means multi-master replication with conflict resolution. Active-passive wastes standby resources but is simpler. The lesson: make your application layer active-active (stateless services behind a load balancer) and accept active-passive for databases unless you have a strong reason to pay the complexity cost of multi-master.
