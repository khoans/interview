**137.** What is a VPN? How does site-to-site VPN differ from client VPN?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
A VPN (Virtual Private Network) creates an encrypted tunnel over the public internet, making remote resources appear as if they are on your local network. Site-to-site VPN connects two networks (e.g., office to AWS VPC) permanently — all traffic between the networks is encrypted. Client VPN connects individual devices to a network — each user establishes their own tunnel.

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, we used both types. AWS Client VPN let individual developers connect their laptops to the VPC for accessing private resources (bastion, internal dashboards, Kibana). Site-to-site VPN connected our office network to the VPC, so office machines could access private resources without individual VPN connections. For accessing production databases, developers had to be on the Client VPN, then SSH through the bastion — two layers of access control. IAM policies on the Client VPN endpoint restricted which subnets each user group could access.

**Trade-off:** Site-to-site VPN is always-on and simpler for users but creates a permanent bridge between networks — if the office network is compromised, the VPC is also exposed. Client VPN is per-user and can enforce MFA per connection, but adds friction for developers. AWS Client VPN costs $0.10/hour per association plus $0.05/hour per connection, which adds up with many developers. We eventually moved most access to Session Manager to reduce VPN dependency and cost.
