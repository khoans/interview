**51.** What is high availability? How do you calculate availability (nines)?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

High availability means a system remains operational for a high percentage of time. Availability is measured in "nines": 99.9% (three nines) = 8.76 hours downtime/year, 99.99% (four nines) = 52.6 minutes/year, 99.999% (five nines) = 5.26 minutes/year. For a chain of dependent services, overall availability is the product: two services at 99.9% each give you 99.8% combined.

In our project, we targeted 99.95% availability for the vehicle search and listing pages — those were revenue-critical. We achieved this through multi-AZ deployment on ECS (instances spread across 3 availability zones), ALB health checks (unhealthy instances removed within 30 seconds), RDS Multi-AZ (automatic database failover), and ElastiCache Redis with automatic failover. Our actual measured availability over a year was 99.97% — the main downtime events were two RDS failovers (~90 seconds each) and one deployment rollback (~3 minutes). We tracked availability in New Relic using synthetic monitoring — pinging critical endpoints every minute from multiple regions.

The trade-off: each additional nine costs exponentially more — going from 99.9% to 99.99% might require multi-region active-active deployment, which doubles infrastructure cost and adds cross-region data replication complexity. We ran active-passive across us-east-1 and us-west-2 rather than active-active because the cost of true active-active wasn't justified for our availability target. The lesson: define your availability target based on business impact, not ego. 99.95% was sufficient — our users could tolerate 4 hours of downtime per year.
