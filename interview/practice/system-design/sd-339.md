**339.** Blue-green vs canary vs rolling deployment: How do your traffic patterns and risk tolerance determine the right strategy?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Use blue-green for instant rollback with zero downtime when you have the budget for double infrastructure. Use canary when you want to test with a small percentage of real traffic before full rollout. Use rolling when you want gradual replacement without extra infrastructure cost.

On our marketplace, we used rolling deployments on ECS — new tasks replaced old tasks one at a time with health checks at each step. This worked well for our Spring Boot services because the deployment was gradual (10-15 minutes for a full rollout) and ECS automatically rolled back if health checks failed. For critical releases affecting search or payment flows, we would have preferred canary — routing 5% of traffic to the new version first, monitoring error rates in New Relic for 15 minutes, then proceeding with full rollout.

The trade-off: blue-green gives instant rollback (just switch the load balancer) but requires 2x infrastructure during deployment — expensive at scale. Canary is the safest for detecting issues with minimal user impact but requires sophisticated traffic splitting and monitoring. Rolling is the cheapest (no extra infrastructure) and simplest but if a bug only appears at full load, you might not catch it until most instances are updated.
