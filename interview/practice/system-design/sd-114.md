**114.** What is PKI (Public Key Infrastructure)? Explain CAs, root certificates, and intermediate certificates.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
PKI is the framework of policies, hardware, software, and procedures for managing digital certificates and public keys. CAs (Certificate Authorities) are trusted entities that issue certificates. Root certificates are self-signed, embedded in OS/browser trust stores. Intermediate certificates are signed by roots and sign end-entity certificates, creating a chain. This separation protects the root key — if an intermediate is compromised, only it is revoked, not the root.

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, the PKI chain for our HTTPS traffic was: Amazon Root CA 1 (pre-installed in browsers) → Amazon Intermediate CA → our server certificate issued by ACM. For internal service-to-service communication, we considered running our own internal CA using AWS Private CA to issue mTLS certificates for microservices. The private CA acted as an intermediate signed by our organization's root, letting us issue short-lived certificates (24-hour validity) for ECS tasks automatically.

**Trade-off:** Running your own private CA gives full control over certificate issuance but requires serious operational investment — key ceremony procedures, HSM for root key storage, CRL/OCSP for revocation. AWS Private CA costs $400/month per CA but eliminates most operational burden. For most teams, using ACM for public certificates and AWS Private CA for internal ones is the pragmatic choice over building custom PKI.
