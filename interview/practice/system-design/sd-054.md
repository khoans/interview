**54.** What is failover? Explain cold, warm, and hot standby.

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

Failover is switching from a failed component to a backup. Cold standby: backup is off, must be started from scratch (minutes to hours). Warm standby: backup is running with data but not serving traffic (seconds to minutes). Hot standby: backup is running, data is synced in real-time, ready to serve immediately (seconds).

In our project, we used different standby levels for different components based on criticality. PostgreSQL RDS Multi-AZ was hot standby — the standby replica received synchronous WAL replication and could be promoted in ~60-90 seconds automatically. Our us-west-2 disaster recovery region was warm standby — infrastructure was provisioned (ECS clusters, ALB configured), database had an async read replica, but services weren't actively running. Switching to it required promoting the replica, updating DNS in Route 53, and starting ECS services — about 15-20 minutes manual process. For non-critical batch processing services (report generation, data export), we had cold standby — just an AMI snapshot that could be launched if needed, taking 10+ minutes.

The trade-off: hot standby is fastest failover but most expensive (duplicate running infrastructure). Cold is cheapest but slowest. We matched the standby level to the service's recovery time objective (RTO): search/listing pages had a 2-minute RTO (hot standby), regional failover had a 30-minute RTO (warm), batch jobs had a 4-hour RTO (cold). The lesson: define your RTO per service, then choose the cheapest standby level that meets it. Don't pay for hot standby on services that can tolerate hours of downtime.
