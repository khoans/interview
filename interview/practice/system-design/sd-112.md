**112.** What is a digital signature? How does it prove authenticity and integrity?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========
A digital signature is created by hashing a message and encrypting the hash with the sender's private key. The recipient decrypts the signature with the sender's public key and compares it to their own hash of the message. If they match, it proves authenticity (only the private key holder could sign it) and integrity (the message was not altered).

**Real example:** On our automotive marketplace, we used RS256 digital signatures in JWT tokens. The auth service signed each token with its RSA private key. When the vehicle-search service received a request with a JWT, it verified the signature using the auth service's public key (fetched from the JWKS endpoint). This proved the token was issued by our auth service (authenticity) and had not been tampered with (integrity) — for example, a user could not modify the "role" claim from "buyer" to "admin" because the signature verification would fail.

**Trade-off:** Digital signatures add computational overhead (RSA signing is expensive, ~1ms per operation). For high-throughput APIs, we cached the JWKS public key and verified locally rather than calling the auth service per request. Also, signatures prove who signed and that data was not altered, but they do not provide confidentiality — the JWT payload is just Base64-encoded, readable by anyone. Encryption is needed for confidentiality.
