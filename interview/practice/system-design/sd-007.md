**7.** What is a reverse proxy? How does it differ from a forward proxy?

========== THIS SECTION IS THE ANSWER ==========

A forward proxy sits in front of clients — clients send requests through it to access the internet (e.g., corporate proxy, VPN). A reverse proxy sits in front of servers — clients don't know which backend server handles their request (e.g., Nginx, ALB, CloudFront). The "reverse" means it's on the server side, not the client side.

In our project, our ALB acted as a reverse proxy — users hit `api.autotrader.com` and the ALB decided which ECS instance handled the request. Users never knew the internal IP of our servers. The ALB also handled SSL termination, so our backend services didn't deal with HTTPS overhead.

The trade-off: a reverse proxy adds a network hop (1-2ms latency) but provides huge benefits: load balancing, SSL termination, request buffering, DDoS protection, and hiding your internal architecture. It's almost always worth it. The only case where you might skip it is for ultra-low-latency internal service-to-service calls where every millisecond matters — but even then, service meshes (Envoy) act as lightweight reverse proxies.
